Perast doesn't try very hard, and that's exactly why it works. There's no old town wall to queue at, no fortress climb to psych yourself up for — just a single row of baroque stone houses along the water, two islands floating just offshore, and a boat that'll take you to one of them for less than the price of a coffee.

## Getting there

Perast sits about 14 km from Kotor, a 20 to 25-minute drive along the bay road depending on summer traffic. From Budva it's closer to 40 minutes via the E80. There's no way to drive into the village itself — cars stop at one of two car parks on either end, both charging around €5-8 for a day ticket in season (free from October through April). If you've already booked a boat tour, ask about the combined parking-plus-boat ticket; it's usually cheaper than paying for both separately.

If you don't want to deal with parking at all, the Blue Line bus runs between Kotor and Perast roughly once an hour, departing Kotor around twenty past the hour, for about €1.50 a ride — though it thins out to every two hours on Sundays, so check before you rely on it. Taxis are an option too, usually somewhere between €10-20 depending on who you flag down, and it's worth agreeing the fare before you get in since meters aren't always used. A rental car is still the easier option if you're planning to string Perast together with Kotor and Budva in the same day, since the bus schedule doesn't leave much room for lingering and it means you're not stuck timing your whole afternoon around an hourly departure.

## The island: Our Lady of the Rocks

This is the reason most people stop in Perast. Gospa od Škrpjela — Our Lady of the Rocks — is a genuinely man-made island, built up stone by stone since 1452 after local sailors reported finding an icon of the Virgin Mary on a reef here. Every July 22nd at sunset, Perast still keeps the tradition alive: townspeople take their boats out and drop rocks into the water around the island, a custom called fašinada that's been going since the very beginning.

Boats leave continuously from the Perast waterfront — no schedule, no booking needed. A round-trip water taxi runs about €5-10 per person, and church entry is roughly €3 on top of that. If you'd rather have someone explain the history as you go, organized boat tours from Kotor that include Perast and the island run from around €15 up to €40-45 if they add stops like the Blue Cave. Buying tickets directly from the boatmen on the Perast pier is usually cheaper than pre-booking, and it's worth agreeing on your return pickup time before you board so you're not standing on the dock waiting.

Inside, the church is small but dense with history: 68 baroque paintings by local artist Tripo Kokolja cover the ceiling and walls, and a side room holds close to 2,500 silver votive tablets left by sailors over the centuries, each one a small thank-you for a safe return home. The visit follows a one-way route through the church, a modest museum, and up to a small gallery — figure on 30 to 45 minutes total, less if you're not lingering over the artwork.

## The island you can't visit

Right next to Our Lady of the Rocks sits its quieter twin, Sveti Đorđe — St. George Island — and this one you can only look at. It's a natural islet with a 12th-century Benedictine monastery and an old cemetery for Perast's noble families, which is how it picked up the local nickname "Island of the Dead." It's been leased to the Diocese of Subotica for decades and stays closed to visitors, so don't plan a stop there — just enjoy it from the boat on your way to the other island. Honestly, the mystery is part of the appeal.

## Perast itself

Don't treat the village as just a boat launch. It's barely 2 km end to end — a 20-minute walk if you don't stop, which you will, because there are 17 baroque palaces and 19 churches packed into a town of fewer than 400 residents. Climb the bell tower of St. Nicholas Church for a proper view over the bay, and budget time for a waterfront lunch; the seafood restaurants along the water aren't cheap by Montenegro standards, but the view of the islands while you eat is hard to put a price on.

Wander a little past the main strip too. The palaces set back from the water — many of them former captains' houses from Perast's shipping days — are worth a slow look even from the outside, and the narrow side streets behind the front row of buildings are where you'll get the quiet-village feeling that the waterfront, full of day-trippers by midday, doesn't really give you.

## When to go

Midday gets crowded, especially once the Kotor cruise groups arrive — the island and the narrow waterfront can feel packed by 11am in peak season. Go early morning or come back in the late afternoon instead, when the light softens and the boat queue disappears. If you're flexible on dates, visiting in June or September gets you the same views with noticeably thinner crowds than July or August.

## Pairing it with the rest of the bay

Perast rarely stands alone on an itinerary, and it doesn't need to. Most people fold it into a longer loop around the Bay of Kotor — Kotor in the morning, Perast around midday or late afternoon, maybe a stop in Risan or Herceg Novi if you're continuing on. If you'd rather not drive the whole loop yourself, hiring a private driver for the day is a common workaround here; it costs more than self-driving but takes the parking headache and the narrow coastal-road stress off your plate entirely, which some people are happy to pay for on a hot July afternoon.

## Bottom line

Give Perast at least half a day — an hour for the island, a couple more for the village and a proper lunch. It pairs naturally with a day based in Kotor, and having your own car makes the whole loop far easier than working around the bus timetable, especially if you're combining it with Budva or other bay stops on the same trip.
