If you've been anywhere near Ulcinj this week, you've probably noticed the town is fuller than usual. EXIT Festival, the long-running electronic music event that spent 25 years in Petrovaradin, Serbia, has relocated its flagship summer edition to Montenegro's coast for 2026 — and it's brought a second event to Budva as well. This is EXIT's biggest move in its 26-year history, and it's happening in real time on beaches most tourists already know well.

## What EXIT to Montenegro actually is

Under the banner "EXIT to Montenegro," the festival is running two separate free weekends this summer. The first, the Long Beach Edition, is taking place right now at Velika Plaža (Long Beach) in Ulcinj, running July 3–6 with warm-up parties that started July 1. The second, the Sea Dance Edition, moves north to Bečići Beach near Budva from August 28–31, with its own warm-up run from August 24–27.

Both events are free to attend, but registration is required in advance through the official site, exit2montenegro.me. If you're planning to catch either one, register before you travel — walk-ups without registration aren't guaranteed entry.

## The Ulcinj edition, happening now

Long Beach stretches roughly 13 kilometers along Montenegro's southernmost coast, just a few miles from the Albanian border, and it's normally known for kitesurfing and low-key beach clubs rather than festival crowds. The Ulcinj lineup has leaned into that setting rather than fighting it — organizers built the weekend around the tagline "No LED, no SFX, no BS," with headliners Argy, Jamie Jones, Maceo Plex, Monolink, Stephan Bodzin, and Awen playing stripped-back sets against sunset rather than a big production stage.

If you're reading this on the festival's last day, the practical move now is simple: get to Long Beach in the afternoon for the sunset sets, and don't expect much parking close to the beach itself — Ulcinj's town center is a 10–15 minute walk from most of the shoreline, and that stretch fills up fast in the final hours of a festival weekend.

## The Sea Dance edition, coming in August

The second weekend is a bigger production. Sea Dance moves to Bečići Beach, right next to Budva — Montenegro's most touristed stretch of coast — from August 28–31, with a lineup that includes Charlotte de Witte, Peggy Gou, Hugel, Enrico Sangiuliano, and John Newman, who's set to debut a new live show. This edition draws on EXIT's classic Dance Arena programming, so expect a heavier, more club-oriented sound than the Ulcinj weekend.

Because Bečići sits inside Budva's regular tourist infrastructure — hotels, restaurants, taxis all a short walk from the beach — this edition is a far easier one to plan a trip around if you're not already based on the coast. Late August is also shoulder-season-adjacent enough that flights and rooms haven't hit the absolute peak pricing of July, though that's likely to shift as the festival gets closer and rooms fill up.

## What it actually costs, even with free entry

"Free" here means the ticket, not the trip. Registration gets you through the gate, but you're still covering flights, accommodation, food, and drinks on-site — and Montenegrin coastal prices climb noticeably during any event that pulls in an international crowd. Beach club areas near the stages tend to charge resort prices for sunbeds and drinks during festival hours, so if you're on a tighter budget, packing your own water and choosing a hotel a short walk rather than a beachfront one can make a real difference over four days.

## Getting there and getting around

Podgorica Airport (TGD) is the main gateway for both events. From the airport, it's about a 1-hour-20-minute drive to Ulcinj and roughly the same to Budva — Ulcinj is the marginally longer of the two by a few minutes. There's no need to route through Podgorica city itself; the airport sits south of town, closer to the coast road.

If you're doing both weekends, or splitting time between the two towns, the coastal drive between Ulcinj and Budva takes about 1 hour 25 minutes. A rental car is genuinely the easier option here — bus connections exist but run on a limited daily schedule, and festival weekends are exactly when you don't want to be stuck waiting on a timetable. Renting for the days around either event gives you the flexibility to chase good weather, beach-hop between sets, and get back to your hotel without relying on festival-exit traffic and a single bus company.

## Where this fits into a wider trip

Neither Ulcinj nor Budva exists only as a festival backdrop, and it's worth building a day or two around each beyond the music. Ulcinj's Old Town — a walled hilltop settlement overlooking the sea — is a quiet contrast to the beach scene below it, and worth an hour's wander in the cooler part of the morning. Budva's own Old Town sits right on the water and gets busy with cruise-ship day-trippers, so early morning or after dinner are the better windows to see it without the crowds.

For anyone building a longer coastal trip around either festival date, this is also peak season for Montenegro generally — sea temperatures are at their warmest of the year, and towns like Kotor and Perast are an easy day trip if you're based anywhere between Budva and the Bay of Kotor. A car makes that kind of day-tripping straightforward in a way that bus timetables generally don't.

## Bottom line

EXIT to Montenegro is a genuinely new event for 2026, not an annual fixture with an established playbook, so registering early and booking accommodation as soon as your dates are fixed matters more than usual — both towns will feel the squeeze as word spreads. If you're catching the tail end of the Ulcinj weekend, get to Long Beach by late afternoon; if you're planning around Budva in August, book rooms sooner rather than later, since Bečići's regular tourist demand will now be stacked on top of festival demand for the same four days.
